When WeeFree exercises don't or won't work

/Stories

The following story is about a young woman who consulted me about a “weak bladder”. Sue (not her real name) was worried about a few episodes of urine leakage a few weeks after the birth of her second child and sensibly decided to consult a pelvic floor physiotherapist.

Sue had experienced a loss of urine on her way to the toilet a couple of times after feeding her baby. Both times she had been very busy during the day and the incontinence episodes occurred in the late afternoon. At the time she also had a cold and had been sneezing a lot.

Of course my first thought was that she may have weak pelvic floor muscles causing some stress incontinence, especially as she revealed that she had difficulty with pelvic floor muscle contractions taught to her postnatally and wasn't sure if she was doing the right thing.

Careful questioning revealed that she had a habit of putting off emptying her bladder and could usually hold on for hours. A full history also revealed a history of constipation and a fear of public toilets. She also had a lot of flatus which caused painful cramps at times, but she was able to control her flatus and never “let go” in public.

An examination of her pelvic floor muscles found that far from having weak pelvic floor muscles, Sue's pelvic floor was in a constant state of tension. She could not feel the muscles contract, because they were already pretty tight. It was a bit like trying to do a biceps curl with your hand strapped to your shoulder - you can't lift if you can let go.

We also found that Sue did not have a good reflex pelvic floor contraction to counteract a cough or sneeze. This is more common than one would think and is particularly common during pregnancy and after childbirth.

So Sue did have stress incontinence, but not because of weak pelvic floor muscles. Instead of her muscles being reactive to the stresses on them and being able to respond to these by contracting when needed, they were in a state of constant tension. This tension was enough to keep her continent most of the time, but in the late afternoon her pelvic floor muscles were in a state of fatigue, with no reserves to counteract a full bladder and repeated stresses from the sneezing.

With help Sue learned to relax her pelvic floor muscles first then how to activate them when needed. Once she was able to release the pelvic floor, she was actually able to increase the strength of the muscles as well.

Her constipation also turned out to be caused by her overactive pelvic floor muscles. The constipation resolved when she learnt correct posture and toilet techniques, and the flatus went away when she stopped taking a lot of fibre supplements.

Sue's story serves as a good reminder that while many people can benefit from self help exercises, not everyone is able to learn how to do them properly on their own and not every case of urinary incontinence is due to weak pelvic floor muscles.

If Sue had carried on ignoring her problem, she could have developed other problems such as anal fissures, pelvic pain and vaginismus. Luckily for her she sought help early.

If you're struggling with your WeeFree exercises or you have other symptoms such as constipation, discomfort during sex or pelvic pain, make sure you consult a physiotherapist or medical professional with training in Pelvic Floor function to get more help.
